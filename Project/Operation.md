# OPERATION

1. A water level indicator is used to monitor and display the level of water in tanks, containers, or other storage systems. It helps in preventing overflow, avoiding water wastage, and ensuring efficient water management in both residential and industrial setups. It is also useful for automating water control systems and protecting pumps from dry running.

2. Water level indicators have a wide range of applications, including use in hotels, home apartments, commercial complexes, and factories. They are also used in cooling towers, residential and commercial swimming pools, and fire protection systems in buildings. Additionally, they can function as liquid level indicators in large containers, fuel level indicators in vehicles, and are commonly used with single-phase motors, submersible pumps, three-phase motors, and open wells where water level monitoring is essential.

3. The working of the water level indicator circuit is based on three main conditions: when the tank is empty, when it is partially filled, and when it is completely full. When there is no water in the tank, no conductive path is formed between the sensing probes and the common probe connected to the 5V supply. Due to this, the transistor remains in the cut-off region as it does not receive sufficient base voltage. The output at the collector remains around 4.2V, which is interpreted as a LOW signal by the microcontroller (operating in active low logic). As a result, the microcontroller displays “0” on the seven-segment display, indicating that the tank is empty.

4. As the water level starts increasing, water acts as a conductive medium and forms a path between the probes and the common probe. This allows the transistors to receive sufficient base bias and start conducting, producing an output voltage (Vce) of approximately 1.2V to 1.8V. The microcontroller, programmed as a priority encoder, detects the highest active input and displays the corresponding water level on the seven-segment display. When the water reaches the second-highest level, a discontinuous buzzer is activated to warn that the tank is about to be full. Finally, when the tank becomes completely full, the top probe is activated, the corresponding transistor conducts, and the microcontroller not only displays the full level but also triggers a continuous buzzer, indicating that the motor should be switched off to prevent overflow.
